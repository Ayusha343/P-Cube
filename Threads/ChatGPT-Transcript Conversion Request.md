One of the earliest technical challenges I faced was related to the bicycle hub and sprocket system. Since this happened a long time ago, I am explaining it based on what I remember.

In a normal bicycle, the rear sprocket is located on the right side, which is the side connected to the pedals. When you pedal, the drivetrain works on that side. The left side of the hub usually remains empty except for the disc brake mount. The disc brake rotor is typically attached to the left side of the hub using screws or threaded mounts.

To utilize that unused side, I decided to use a hub that had threads on both sides. I purchased a double-threaded hub from a shop, which meant that both the left and right sides had threaded mounts.

However, there was a problem. The left side thread is usually a reverse thread. This means that when the component rotates in a certain direction, it tends to loosen instead of tightening. On the right side, when you pedal, the rotation actually tightens the sprocket. But on the left side, using the same concept causes the component to gradually loosen while rotating.

To solve this, our first idea was to add washers on the left side to prevent the component from loosening. However, this solution failed completely because the assembly started slipping and did not hold properly.

The next idea was to weld the component directly. But welding would create new problems. It would make maintenance very difficult, and there was also a risk of damaging the hub due to heat. If the hub overheated, it could damage the ball bearings or cause them to seize.

We also considered machining the hub using a lathe to slightly modify its size, although that part is a separate story.

Eventually, we attached a freewheel to the hub using Araldite two-part epoxy. This created a very strong bond, almost similar to a welded joint, and provided a solid connection between the components.

The motor mounting mechanism was designed in a way that the bicycle frame was placed in the middle, and the mounting plates held the frame from both sides in a sandwich-like configuration.

Two soft iron plates, approximately 2–3 mm thick, were used on both sides of the frame. These plates were tightened together so that pressure from both sides would clamp the motor firmly onto the bicycle frame.

However, the motor sometimes slipped due to insufficient friction. To solve this, pieces of old bicycle rubber tube were inserted between the bracket and the frame to increase friction and prevent slipping.

The mounting used an M4 screw approximately 10 cm long, along with TBS brand nuts and bolts.

Another technical challenge involved the battery configuration. Each battery was rated at 12V, and two batteries were used to create a 24V system.

The initial plan was to charge the batteries in series using a 24V charger (which typically outputs around 30V). However, this approach required a battery management system (BMS), which would increase the overall cost. There was also a risk of voltage imbalance between the batteries over time.

To avoid this, I designed the system so that the motor would run in a 24V series configuration, but the batteries would be charged in parallel at 12V.

This required a series-to-parallel switching mechanism. Initially, I planned to use a DPDT switch to perform this conversion.

However, the issue was that a DPDT switch capable of handling high DC current (around 15–20 amps) was difficult to find at a reasonable price. Most suitable switches were priced in the range of ₹1000–₹2000.

As an alternative, I attempted to use two SPDT switches to perform the same function.

Unfortunately, both switches eventually melted due to the high current. The internal plastic components melted, and the internal contact ball even fused due to overheating.

Because of this failure, I removed the switches entirely.

Instead, I used two or three 2-pin female plug sockets and configured them to function similarly to a DPDT switching system.

The wiring was connected from the back, and two very thick wires were attached at the front to act as the current path. I am not sure about the exact wire gauge, but they were very thick wires that I had available at home. They effectively handled the high current.

This solution was somewhat of a temporary “jugaad” workaround. It does not look very refined, but when purchasing components in bulk later, a proper high-current DPDT switch could be used.

Later, I also needed to install a voltage sensor and a voltmeter. That system also required a DPDT switch.

I did find a suitable DPDT switch, but while soldering the connections, one of its pins broke.

So I modified the design again. I used an SPDT switch and made the rest of the wiring common negative, adding another switch to perform the dual function.

There was also a change in the battery selection. Initially, I planned to use a motorcycle lead-acid battery.

However, it turned out to be too large and heavy. Therefore, I switched to sealed lead-acid batteries, the type commonly used in UPS systems. These batteries are smaller, maintenance-free, and easier to integrate into the design.

The pipe and bearing assembly were mounted to the frame using hose clamps. This created a simple tensioner mechanism that maintained proper chain tension and prevented the chain from slipping off.

Another important discovery was related to hybrid operation. Initially, I believed that either pedaling or motor assistance should be used at a time, assuming that using both simultaneously would stress the motor.

However, after one to two weeks of experimentation, I realized that both could be used simultaneously without issues. Pedaling together with motor assistance provided torque assist and significantly improved acceleration.

Several component failures occurred over time, mostly on the mechanical side.

On the electrical side, the primary failure involved the LED lights. The brake light switch also failed multiple times because it was a small component, and its solder joints would break due to vibration.

The lights also suffered from UV exposure because the bicycle was parked outside at school, leading to gradual degradation.

Brake pads naturally required periodic replacement as part of normal wear and tear.

The accelerator failed once after approximately two years of operation and had to be replaced.

The battery also had to be replaced once during the two-year period. This occurred because I accidentally overused and deeply discharged the lead-acid battery. At the time, I was not aware that excessive deep discharge significantly shortens the lifespan of lead-acid batteries.

*   Motor-only top speed reached about **25 km/h**.
