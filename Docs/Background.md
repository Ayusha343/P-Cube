# Background Story

## The Motivation Behind the Idea

The story of the P Cube project began in 9th grade when my family shifted to a house approximately two kilometers away from my school. For the first year and a half, I commuted by walking to school, sometimes running, and occasionally borrowing friends' bicycles when available. This experience highlighted the challenge of daily commuting in an urban environment and the value of personal transportation.

When I entered 10th grade, my mother purchased a simple, thin bicycle for me. I used this bicycle regularly to commute to school, which solved the immediate transportation problem. However, the physical exertion required for the 2 km journey, especially on days with adverse weather or when feeling unwell, remained a concern.

After completing my 10th grade board exams, I had a gap of approximately one and a half months before 11th grade commenced. During this time, rather than remaining idle, I decided to undertake a meaningful project. The idea of converting my bicycle into an electric-assisted vehicle seemed ideal—it would combine practical utility with hands-on learning.

## Initial Research and Realization

When I began researching electric bicycle conversion kits available in the market, I discovered a significant barrier: cost. Even the most basic conversion kits were priced between ₹12,000 and ₹15,000. These kits often lacked essential components such as batteries, chargers, and protective covers, leading to substantial hidden costs when fully assembling a functional system.

In contrast, fully built electric bicycles were priced upwards of ₹30,000, making them inaccessible to students and low-income users. This pricing gap presented an opportunity: instead of purchasing a ready-made kit, I could source individual components and assemble them myself, potentially achieving both a working electric bicycle and significant cost savings.

## Conceptual Development and Planning

To develop this idea further, I discussed the concept with my elder brother, who was studying in college at the time. Through WhatsApp conversations, we discussed fundamental design aspects: how the motor would be mounted, how the battery would be positioned and secured, how power transmission would work, and how the overall system architecture would be organized. My brother's input provided valuable perspective on mechanical design principles.

I spent considerable time researching different motor types, battery configurations, and mounting mechanisms. I compared specifications and prices of components available both online and offline, creating a comprehensive parts list and cost estimate. This research phase took several weeks, as reliable information required cross-referencing multiple sources.

Sourcing all components individually was time-consuming. Components were ordered from different vendors—some from online retailers and others from local shops—each with varying lead times and availability. The process of collecting all parts before assembly began took several months.
